How to Prepare Your Oil Tank for Winter

By Oil Tanks Plus 4 min read September 2024

Cold weather is consistently the busiest time for emergency call-outs to oil-heated properties. Most of those call-outs are for problems that could have been prevented with a simple autumn check. Here's what to do before the first frost hits.

1. Check Your Fuel Level Now — Don't Wait for Winter

Oil prices tend to rise in autumn and peak in mid-winter when demand surges. If you wait until November or December to order, you'll almost certainly pay more per litre, and delivery slots can be 2–3 weeks out during cold snaps.

The best time to order your winter supply is September or October, when prices are typically lower and delivery times are short. Aim to have at least a quarter-tank heading into winter, and ideally fill up before the end of October.

Most tanks have a gauge — check it, and if it's reading below 25%, order now.

2. Lag Exposed Pipework

Oil doesn't freeze at typical UK winter temperatures, but water can. If water has entered your system via condensation or a fitting failure, frozen water in pipework can cause the heating to fail or, worse, split a pipe. More importantly, the oil supply line itself can become sluggish as the oil's viscosity increases in very cold temperatures.

Any exposed oil pipework — particularly on north-facing walls or in exposed outbuildings — should be lagged with foam pipe insulation. This is an inexpensive, half-day job that can prevent expensive emergency call-outs.

3. Check the Fire Valve

Your tank outlet should have an automatic fire valve (AFV) that cuts the oil supply in the event of a fire. In cold weather, these can occasionally stick in the closed position — cutting off the fuel supply for no fire-related reason. If your heating cuts out suddenly in cold weather and there's no obvious cause, check the fire valve first. It's usually a small inline valve close to the tank outlet.

4. Clear Vegetation and Debris Around the Tank

Autumn leaves and debris can pile up around a tank and trap moisture, accelerating external corrosion on fittings and steel tanks. Clear any build-up, and trim back any overhanging vegetation. This also improves access for the delivery driver and makes it easier to spot any developing problems.

5. Check for Water in the Tank

Condensation can cause small amounts of water to accumulate at the base of your oil tank over time. In a cold winter, this water can freeze and cause problems. It also promotes bacterial growth (known as diesel bug) that can clog filters and damage your boiler.

Signs of water contamination include unusually frequent boiler lock-outs, a cloudy appearance to the oil, or a jelly-like sludge in the filter. If you suspect water contamination, fuel polishing can resolve it — call us on 0333 222 4037.

6. Test Your Boiler Before You Need It

Don't wait for the first cold night of autumn to discover your boiler isn't working. Switch the heating on in September and run it for an hour. If it fires up and runs cleanly, great. If there are any issues — unusual noises, lock-outs, poor heat output — you'll have time to get an engineer out before the cold sets in.

7. Know Where Your Shut-Off Valve Is

If something goes wrong — a pipe splits, a fitting fails, an oil smell develops — you need to be able to cut off the oil supply quickly. Find the shut-off valve (typically on the oil line near the tank or near the boiler) and make sure everyone in the household knows where it is.

Winter Checklist Summary
  • Order oil before end of October
  • Lag exposed pipework
  • Check and test the fire valve
  • Clear debris from around the tank
  • Check for water/sludge contamination
  • Test the boiler before it's needed
  • Locate the shut-off valve
