The Real Downsides of Air Source Heat Pumps

By Oil Tanks Plus 7 min read October 2024

Air source heat pumps are promoted as the future of home heating — and for the right property, they can be an excellent choice. But for many UK homes, particularly older rural properties that currently heat with oil, the reality is more nuanced. Here's an honest look at the limitations before you make the switch.

1. The Upfront Cost Is Substantial

A full air source heat pump installation — including the unit, a new or upgraded hot water cylinder, radiator upgrades where needed and the required electrical work — typically costs £10,000–£18,000. The government's Boiler Upgrade Scheme (BUS) currently offers a £7,500 grant, but this is subject to income thresholds, eligibility checks, and has capacity limits with no guarantee of future availability.

By contrast, a new oil-fired boiler with a replacement tank runs from around £4,000–£6,000 all-in — and your existing infrastructure stays largely unchanged.

2. Older Homes Often Need Significant Upgrades

Heat pumps operate most efficiently at lower flow temperatures (35–45°C) than oil boilers (70–80°C). This means they need larger heat emitters — typically underfloor heating or oversized radiators — to heat your home at those lower temperatures.

In a Victorian farmhouse or a traditional stone cottage, replacing radiators throughout the property is a major disruption. In properties with solid walls and limited insulation, it may not be cost-effective at all without first spending significantly on insulation.

3. Performance Drops in Cold Weather

Heat pumps extract heat from the outside air, and their efficiency (measured as COP — coefficient of performance) drops as temperatures fall. In mild UK winters, a heat pump can deliver 3–4 units of heat for every unit of electricity consumed. But when temperatures drop below freezing — exactly when you need the most heat — efficiency falls, and some units switch to a less efficient backup electric heater.

Oil boilers are not affected by outside temperatures. An oil boiler works just as well in January as it does in October.

4. Running Costs Depend Heavily on Electricity Prices

The economics of a heat pump vs oil boiler depend almost entirely on the relative cost of electricity vs heating oil at any given time. Electricity in the UK currently costs around 24p/kWh, while heating oil (at recent prices) is equivalent to around 7–10p/kWh equivalent.

Even at a COP of 3, a heat pump's effective energy cost is around 8p/kWh — competitive with oil when oil prices are high, but less so when oil prices are lower. And when a heat pump switches to its electric backup in extreme cold, the cost rises further.

The government has pledged to reduce the electricity/gas price ratio through policy changes, but no date has been given.

5. Installation Is Disruptive

A heat pump installation typically takes 2–4 days and involves significant disruption — especially if underfloor heating is being added or radiators are being upgraded throughout the property. External works, electrical upgrades and hot water cylinder replacement all add to the disruption.

An oil tank replacement, by comparison, is typically completed in half a day with minimal disruption to your home.

6. Not All Properties Are Suitable

Heat pumps work best in well-insulated, modern properties. If your property has an EPC rating below D, the Energy Performance Certificate itself may flag that a heat pump is not the most appropriate solution. Properties in very exposed rural locations — particularly at altitude — also present greater challenges due to the combination of lower temperatures and higher heat demand.

An Honest View

We install oil tanks, so we have an obvious perspective here. But we also believe in giving people accurate information. Heat pumps are the right solution for some properties. For older rural homes currently on oil, a modern oil system is often the most practical and cost-effective heating solution available today — and may remain so for the foreseeable future.

Oil Heating in 2025 and Beyond

The government's current position is that new oil boiler installations will not be banned in off-gas-grid properties before 2035 at the earliest. There remains significant uncertainty about the timeline and scope of any future restrictions. Modern oil heating systems are increasingly efficient, and the development of renewable liquid fuels (including HVO — hydrotreated vegetable oil) means that oil infrastructure may continue to be relevant well into the future.
