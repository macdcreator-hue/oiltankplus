Buying a House with an Oil Tank? Read This First

By Oil Tanks Plus 6 min read November 2024

Roughly 1.5 million properties in the UK heat with oil, most of them in rural areas beyond the gas network. Buying one of these properties is straightforward in most cases — but the oil tank itself deserves careful attention before you exchange contracts.

Why the Oil Tank Matters in a House Purchase

The oil tank is a piece of infrastructure that, if it fails, can cost tens of thousands of pounds to remediate. Oil contamination of soil or groundwater carries serious environmental liability — and as the new property owner, that liability transfers to you. An old, uncertified or non-compliant tank is worth factoring into your negotiation, or requesting replacement as a condition of sale.

10 Questions to Ask the Seller

  1. How old is the tank? Look for a date stamp on the plastic body (usually moulded near the base or under a fitting). Tanks over 15 years old should be treated with caution.
  2. Is it bunded? A single-skin tank near a watercourse or in a sensitive area may not be compliant. Ask if there's a bund — or look for the tell-tale double-walled design.
  3. Has it ever leaked? Sellers have a legal obligation to disclose known issues. If there's been a leak, you want to know about contamination.
  4. Is there an OFTEC certificate? Any post-2005 installation should have one. If none exists, the installation was either pre-2005 or carried out without proper certification.
  5. When was the last professional inspection? An annual check is recommended. Gaps in inspection history increase risk.
  6. Has it had fuel polishing? Older tanks sometimes accumulate sludge and water at the base. Fuel polishing clears this, but if it hasn't been done in years, the heating system may have debris in the oil line.
  7. What's the capacity? Confirm it's appropriate for the size of the property. Under-sized tanks mean more frequent deliveries and less flexibility on pricing.
  8. What's the current oil level? A negotiating point — oil in the tank has real monetary value.
  9. Are there any active issues with the heating? Boiler problems are often tank-related.
  10. What's the fuel delivery access like? Delivery tankers need adequate access. Restricted access properties can face a premium on deliveries.

What to Check on a Site Visit

When you visit the property, take a few minutes to check the tank directly:

  • Walk around it and look for cracks, crazing, staining or visible leaks
  • Check the base — it should be solid, level and non-combustible
  • Look at the fittings — any weeping or staining around the outlet or vent is a warning sign
  • Is it sited an appropriate distance from buildings, boundaries and watercourses? (See our regulations guide)
  • Is there vegetation or debris growing around the tank? This can trap moisture and accelerate corrosion

Non-Compliant Tanks: Your Liability After Purchase

If the tank is found to be non-compliant after you purchase — for example, a single-skin tank in a location that now requires bunding — you as the new owner are responsible for bringing it into compliance. Negotiating on the Oil Tank

If the tank is old, damaged or non-compliant, you have a few options:

  • Negotiate a price reduction to cover replacement cost
  • Request the seller replace the tank before completion
  • Accept the property at the existing price with the intention of budgeting for replacement

Replacement typically costs £1,200–£2,500 for a standard domestic bunded tank. Get a quote from us before you negotiate — knowing the actual cost puts you in a stronger position.

What Happens If the Tank Is Fine?

If the tank is relatively modern, bunded, certificated and in good visible condition, there's generally nothing to worry about. Arrange an inspection by an OFTEC engineer in the first year of ownership to establish a baseline, and schedule annual checks going forward. Oil heating is reliable, cost-effective and gives you independence from the gas grid — when the infrastructure is maintained, it rarely causes problems.
