Bunded vs Single-Skin Oil Tanks: Which Do You Really Need?

By Oil Tanks Plus 6 min read January 2025

When it comes to replacing or installing a new oil tank, the choice between bunded and single-skin is one of the first decisions you'll need to make. It affects your cost, your legal compliance, and your environmental liability for years to come. Here's everything you need to know.

What Is a Single-Skin Oil Tank?

A single-skin tank is exactly what it sounds like — a tank made from a single layer of polyethylene or steel. It holds the heating oil directly with no secondary containment around it. Single-skin tanks are the traditional option and are still perfectly legal in many circumstances.

Single-skin tanks are lighter, slightly less expensive, and easier to install in some locations. However, if the tank develops a crack, leak, or fitting failure, there's nothing to catch the oil before it reaches the ground.

What Is a Bunded Oil Tank?

A bunded tank is essentially a tank within a tank. The inner tank holds the oil, while an outer shell — known as the bund — acts as a secondary containment layer. The bund is typically designed to hold at least 110% of the inner tank's capacity, ensuring that even a complete failure of the inner tank won't result in an environmental spill.

Modern bunded tanks are manufactured as a single unit — you're not buying two separate tanks. The gap between the inner and outer walls typically includes a space for monitoring, and many come with an integrated contents gauge and overfill prevention device.

When Are You Legally Required to Have a Bunded Tank?

This is where it gets important. Under the Control of Pollution (Oil Storage) Regulations and OFTEC guidelines, a bunded tank is legally required if any of the following apply:

  • The tank holds more than 2,500 litres
  • The tank is located within 10 metres of a watercourse (stream, river, pond, ditch, drain)
  • The tank is within 50 metres of a borehole or spring
  • The site is in a Source Protection Zone (SPZ)
  • The tank is installed for commercial, agricultural or industrial use
  • Oil would reach a loose-surfaced road or drain if a spill occurred

If none of these apply and you're replacing an existing domestic tank under 2,500 litres, a single-skin is technically permitted — though we always recommend bunded for peace of mind.

OFTEC Registered Engineers Advise This

Even where a bunded tank isn't legally required, our OFTEC-registered engineers recommend one in most cases. The additional cost is modest, and the protection against ground contamination, insurance claims and legal liability is significant.

Cost Difference: Bunded vs Single-Skin

Bunded tanks typically cost 30–50% more than equivalent single-skin models at the point of supply. However, the installation labour is broadly the same, and the ongoing peace of mind — and reduced insurance risk — often justifies the difference.

For context, a 1,000-litre single-skin tank might cost £400–£600 to supply, while an equivalent bunded tank might cost £600–£900. Installation costs are typically £300–£600 on top of that, regardless of tank type.

When you factor in that a single leak can cost tens of thousands of pounds in environmental remediation — costs that insurance may not cover — the bunded option is almost always the better long-term investment.

Does a Bunded Tank Need Planning Permission?

Neither bunded nor single-skin tanks typically require planning permission for domestic replacements, as they generally fall under permitted development rights. Exceptions include listed buildings, conservation areas, and tanks in some locations that exceed certain dimensions. See our full guide to planning permission for oil tanks.

Which Should You Choose?

Our recommendation, based on 25+ years of oil tank installations:

  • Always choose bunded if you're legally required to — there's no choice here
  • Choose bunded if your property is near any watercourse, drain or public road
  • Choose bunded if your property is in a rural area with a private water supply
  • Consider single-skin only for like-for-like replacement of a small domestic tank in an open, well-drained location away from any watercourse

In practice, the vast majority of our installations — over 80% — are bunded tanks. The regulations have been moving in this direction for years, and for good reason.
