Do You Need Planning Permission for an Oil Tank?

By Oil Tanks Plus 3 min read August 2024

One of the most common questions we're asked when someone is planning an oil tank installation: "Do I need planning permission?" In most cases, the answer is no — but there are important exceptions.

The Short Answer: Usually Not

For the majority of domestic oil tank installations and replacements in England, oil tanks fall under permitted development rights — meaning planning permission is not required, provided certain conditions are met.

The key permitted development rules for oil tanks (under Schedule 2, Part 1, Class H of the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) Order 2015) are:

  • The tank capacity does not exceed 3,500 litres
  • The tank is not positioned in front of the principal elevation of the house (i.e. the front of the property)
  • The tank is not within the curtilage of a listed building
  • The installation does not bring the total fuel storage on the property to over 3,500 litres

If all of these conditions are met, you can install or replace an oil tank without applying for planning permission.

When You Do Need Planning Permission

Planning permission is required in the following circumstances:

  • Listed buildings: any tank within the curtilage of a listed building requires listed building consent and may also require planning permission
  • Conservation areas: stricter rules apply in conservation areas — check with your Local Planning Authority (LPA)
  • Tanks over 3,500 litres: larger domestic or agricultural tanks require planning permission
  • Front of property positioning: if the only suitable location is visible from the road at the front of the property, planning permission is needed
  • Scotland and Wales: the rules vary slightly — consult your local planning authority

Do I Need Planning Permission for a Bunded Tank?

The planning rules apply equally to both bunded and single-skin tanks — the type of tank doesn't affect whether planning permission is needed. What matters is the capacity, the location, and whether the property has any designations (listed, conservation area, etc.).

However, remember that environmental regulations (separate from planning) may require you to have a bunded tank regardless of your planning situation. See our guide to bunded vs single-skin tanks for a full explanation.

Building Regulations — Separate from Planning

Even where planning permission isn't required, Building Regulations do apply to oil tank installations. This covers siting distances, pipework, fire valves and the requirement for an OFTEC certificate.

An OFTEC-registered engineer (like all of our engineers) self-certifies the work as compliant with Building Regulations, notifying your Local Authority on your behalf. You don't need to submit a separate Building Regulations application when using a registered installer.

What About Flats and Apartments?

Permitted development rights don't apply to flats and apartments. If you're in a flat, you'd need to apply for planning permission — though in practice, oil tanks in flats are extremely unusual.
