The Complete Guide to Oil Tank Replacement

By Oil Tanks Plus 8 min read December 2024

Whether your tank is ageing, damaged, or you're upgrading to a more suitable specification, replacing an oil tank is a straightforward process when you know what to expect. This guide covers everything — from deciding when to replace, to choosing the right tank, to what happens on installation day.

When Should You Replace Your Oil Tank?

There's no single answer, but these are the most common triggers for tank replacement:

  • Age: most plastic tanks are designed to last 10–20 years. If yours is approaching or past 15 years old, replacement is worth planning proactively
  • Visible damage: cracks, crazing, bulging, or visible corrosion on a steel tank
  • Leaks or oil smell: any sign of oil leakage requires immediate attention
  • Regulations change: if you're now required to have a bunded tank (e.g. due to proximity to water) but don't have one
  • Change of use: upgrading from domestic to commercial, or requiring a larger capacity
  • Failed inspection: an OFTEC engineer has advised replacement following assessment

Step 1: Choose the Right Replacement Tank

Tank choice depends on three main factors: your legal requirements, the available space, and your heating demand.

Bunded or Single-Skin?

For most domestic replacements, we recommend a bunded tank even where it isn't legally required. The additional cost is modest, and the protection against ground contamination and legal liability is significant. See our bunded vs single-skin comparison guide.

What Size Do You Need?

The most common domestic tank size is 1,000–1,250 litres. This is enough for most average-sized homes. If you're in a larger property, or you want to buy oil in bulk when prices are lower, a 1,500–2,500 litre tank may be more cost-effective long-term. Larger tanks mean fewer deliveries and more flexibility on when you buy.

Plastic or Steel?

Plastic bunded tanks are the most common choice for domestic installations — they're lower maintenance, resistant to external corrosion and typically carry a longer warranty. Steel tanks are preferred where space constraints require a fire-rated option, or where you want the longest possible service life.

Step 3: What Happens on Installation Day

A standard domestic replacement typically takes half a day. Here's what happens:

  1. Residual oil transfer: any remaining oil in the old tank is pumped out and either transferred to a temporary container or into the new tank once installed
  2. Old tank removal: we safely disconnect and remove the old tank, which we take for responsible recycling. Zero landfill policy
  3. Base check: we check the existing base is suitable. If it needs work, this is flagged during the survey
  4. New tank positioning: the new tank is manoeuvred into position. Large tanks may require a crane or pump truck
  5. Pipework and fittings: oil lines are connected, a fire valve fitted, and all joints checked for integrity
  6. Commission and test: we fill the tank, bleed the system if needed, and test the supply
  7. OFTEC certificate: we issue your installation certificate before we leave
