UK Oil Tank Installation Regulations: A Plain-English Guide

By Oil Tanks Plus 5 min read March 2025

Oil tank installations in the UK are governed by a combination of British Standards, OFTEC regulations, and environmental legislation. Get it wrong, and you could face a fine, an insurance claim rejection, or worse — an environmental prosecution. Here's what you need to know.

The Key Regulations That Apply

Three main pieces of legislation govern domestic and commercial oil tank installation in the UK:

  • BS 5410: Parts 1 & 2 — the British Standard for oil-fired heating installations, covering tank siting, pipework, and safety requirements
  • The Control of Pollution (Oil Storage) Regulations 2001 (England) — sets out requirements for secondary containment (bunding) and structural integrity
  • OFTEC Technical Books (TB117, TB101) — OFTEC guidance documents used alongside Building Regulations Part J

Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland have separate but broadly similar regulations. Our engineers work across all nations and are familiar with the local requirements in each.

OFTEC Registration: Why It Matters

An OFTEC-registered engineer is to oil heating what a Gas Safe engineer is to gas. Oil tank installation must be carried out by an OFTEC-registered technician to be legally compliant in England, Wales and Northern Ireland. In Scotland, an equivalent competence is required under HETAS.

When our engineers complete an installation, they issue an OFTEC installation certificate (Form CD10 or CD11). This is important for insurance purposes and is often required when you sell your property. We issue it on the day — no chasing, no delays.

Distance and Siting Requirements

Where your tank can be positioned is strictly regulated. Under BS 5410 and OFTEC guidance, the minimum distances are:

  • 1.8 metres from any non-fire-rated building opening (door, window)
  • 760mm from a fire-rated wall or structure
  • 760mm from the boundary of your property
  • 1.8 metres from a non-fire-rated boundary fence or structure
  • 600mm minimum on all sides for maintenance access

There are exceptions if you use a fire-rated tank (e.g. an Alufire or steel fire-rated model) — these can be sited much closer to structures. Speak to our engineers about options if space is limited.

When a Bunded Tank Is Legally Required

The Control of Pollution (Oil Storage) Regulations require a bunded tank if any of the following apply:

  • The tank holds more than 2,500 litres
  • It is within 10 metres of a watercourse (river, stream, ditch, drain)
  • It is within 50 metres of a spring or borehole
  • The site is in a Source Protection Zone
  • The installation is for commercial, agricultural or industrial use

Even where a bunded tank isn't strictly required, we recommend one for most domestic installations. See our full guide to bunded vs single-skin tanks.

Tank Base and Support Requirements

Your tank must sit on a stable, non-combustible base capable of supporting its full weight when filled. A 1,000-litre tank full of oil weighs approximately 900kg plus the tank itself. For plastic tanks, a purpose-made bunded base or a concrete plinth is recommended. Steel tanks can sit on purpose-made legs or a solid concrete base.

The base must not slope towards any watercourse or drain. If your proposed location doesn't have a suitable base, we can arrange for one to be prepared as part of the installation.

Pipework Regulations

Oil feed pipes must be fire-valve protected — an automatic fire valve (AFV) must be fitted close to the tank outlet to cut fuel supply in the event of a fire. This is mandatory. All pipework must be inspected and, where underground, suitably protected from mechanical damage and corrosion.

Notification Requirements

New oil tank installations must be notified to your Local Authority Building Control (LABC) or a competent person scheme. An OFTEC-registered engineer self-certifies the work through OFTEC, meaning you don't need a separate building regulations application — OFTEC handles the notification on your behalf.

What Happens If You Don't Comply?

An uncertified installation can invalidate your home insurance, create problems when you sell, and expose you to Environment Agency prosecution if a spill occurs. Local authorities can also issue enforcement notices requiring remediation work at your cost. The penalty for an oil spill can run to thousands of pounds in clean-up costs alone.

Compliance isn't just a legal formality — it protects you, your neighbours and the environment.
